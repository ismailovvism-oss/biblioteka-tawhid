**Chapter Two. On Note-Taking**

<!-- s002 -->
Notes are not a copy of the book; they are a record of the encounter between the book and its reader. Two readers of the same volume should produce different notebooks.[^1]

<!-- s003 -->
Write the author's claim in the author's words, and your objection in your own. Mixing the two voices on one line is how libraries of misquotation are born.

<!-- s004 -->
A note without a source is a rumor addressed to your future self. Date it, page it, and your future self will thank you; omit these, and the note decays into "I read somewhere that..."[^2]

<!-- s005 -->
The notebook closes; the habit remains. In the end that habit — pausing, weighing, restating — is the only note that cannot be lost.

<!-- fn1 -->
This is why borrowed summaries serve so poorly: the encounter they record is someone else's.

<!-- fn2 -->
Archivists call the missing data "provenance". A claim travels on its provenance the way a traveler travels on documents.
