**Chapter One. On Reading**

<!-- s002 -->
A book is read twice: once with the eyes and once with the mind. The first reading collects the words; the second weighs them.[^1]

<!-- s003 -->
Readers who hurry through a difficult passage gain nothing but the illusion of progress. The passage remains where it was, unread, waiting for their return.

<!-- s004 -->
It is said that al-Khalil ibn Ahmad remarked: "The best knowledge is that which you hold present in your mind, not that which sits in your books."[^2]

<!-- p2 -->
<!-- s005 -->
A slow reader with a pencil outreads a fast reader with none. What the pencil touches, the memory keeps; what the eye merely passes over, the week erases.

<!-- s006 -->
Therefore make your reading small and your re-reading large. One page truly owned is worth a chapter borrowed.[^3]

<!-- fn1 -->
The distinction is old. Ancient grammarians separated *riwaya* — transmission of a text — from *diraya* — comprehension of it. A reader may possess the first in perfection and the second not at all.
